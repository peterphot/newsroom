## Voice Summary

Two or three sentences capturing the essence of this voice. What makes it distinctive? What would someone immediately notice on the page?

Example: "Sharp, data-led prose with short, declarative sentences. Jane writes like a frustrated insider — confident the reader can keep up, impatient with the genre's clichés. Openings are provocative, never scene-setting; conclusions land on a single sharp implication."

## Detailed Style Notes

Structured notes a writer can reference while drafting. Cover these dimensions:

- **Sentence structure patterns** — Predominantly short and punchy? Long and complex? Varied with rhythm? Be specific.
- **Vocabulary level** — Technical depth, jargon usage, accessibility. Does the writer assume specialist knowledge or explain terms?
- **Use of data vs anecdote** — How does the writer use evidence? Heavy on statistics, anecdote-driven, or a mix?
- **Rhetorical devices** — Questions to the reader, metaphors, repetition, lists, direct address, irony, understatement.
- **Formality level** — Formal/academic, conversational, colloquial. Where on the spectrum?
- **Use of metaphor and imagery** — Frequent or rare? What kinds (sporting, military, domestic, technical)?
- **Paragraph length tendencies** — Short paragraphs (1-2 sentences) or longer blocks? How does length vary?
- **Transitions between ideas** — Explicit transition phrases, abrupt shifts, logical connectives, whitespace?
- **Opening patterns** — Hooks, cold opens, provocative statements, anecdotes, data points, questions?
- **Closing patterns** — Calls to action, synthesis, open questions, callbacks to the opening, forward-looking statements?

## Do's and Don'ts

Specific, actionable guidance for emulating this voice.

### Do

- Open with a bold, falsifiable claim, not a scene-setter.
- Use one-sentence paragraphs to land emphasis.
- Attribute every statistic on first mention.

### Don't

- Don't use the passive voice for attribution ("It is said that..." → "Smith argues that...").
- Don't pad transitions ("As we will see...", "It is important to note...").
- Don't end on a hedge ("Only time will tell.").
