# Trade Media Article

## Overview

A trade media article is a long-form opinion or analysis piece written for an industry-specific audience. It differs from general business journalism in that it assumes the reader has domain knowledge, cares about practical implications, and values evidence over narrative. The tone is authoritative but accessible, evidence-based rather than sensationalist.

**Typical word count:** 800-1500 words.

---

## Structural Template

### 1. Hook/Lede
<!-- advisory-only -->

**Purpose:** The opening that grabs the reader's attention and signals what the piece is about and why it matters right now.

**Typical length:** 1-2 paragraphs (roughly 10% of total word count).

**What makes it good:**
- Starts with a concrete, timely observation -- a data point, an industry event, a provocative claim
- Creates urgency: the reader understands immediately why this matters now
- Avoids throat-clearing or generic scene-setting

**What makes it bad:**
- Opens with a dictionary definition or broad historical sweep
- Buries the point under vague setup
- Uses clickbait that the article cannot deliver on

**Example opening patterns:**
- Data-led: "Last quarter, X fell by 30% -- and most of the industry hasn't noticed yet."
- Event-led: "When [Company] announced [move] last week, the reaction was [X]. But the real story is [Y]."
- Provocation-led: "The conventional wisdom about [topic] is wrong, and the data proves it."

---

### 2. Context
<!-- advisory-only -->

**Purpose:** Situates the argument in the broader industry landscape. Gives the reader enough background to follow the thesis, without over-explaining what they already know.

**Typical length:** 1-2 paragraphs (roughly 10-15% of total word count).

**What makes it good:**
- Quickly establishes the status quo or prevailing narrative
- References recent industry trends, shifts, or events the audience is tracking
- Creates a clear bridge from the lede to the core argument

**What makes it bad:**
- Rehashes well-known history the audience already knows
- Goes too broad -- loses the thread that connects to the argument
- Reads like a textbook rather than an informed perspective

**Transition phrases:**
- "This isn't happening in isolation."
- "To understand why this matters, consider..."
- "The shift has been building for some time."

---

### 3. Argument
<!-- advisory-only -->

**Purpose:** The core thesis of the piece, clearly and directly stated. This is the single idea the article exists to advance.

**Typical length:** 1 paragraph (roughly 5-10% of total word count).

**What makes it good:**
- States the thesis in one or two sentences that could stand alone
- Is specific and falsifiable -- not a vague truism
- Takes a clear position that some readers might disagree with

**What makes it bad:**
- Is wishy-washy or hedged to the point of saying nothing
- Requires three paragraphs to state
- Is obvious or uncontroversial (if everyone agrees, why write it?)

**Example argument patterns:**
- "[Industry practice] is fundamentally broken because [reason], and the companies that recognise this first will [outcome]."
- "The data suggests that [trend] isn't what it appears -- it's actually [reframing]."

---

### 4. Evidence
<!-- advisory-only -->

**Purpose:** Data, examples, and research that support the argument. This is where the piece earns its credibility.

**Typical length:** 3-5 paragraphs (roughly 30-35% of total word count).

**What makes it good:**
- Uses specific data points with clear attribution (source, date, methodology where relevant)
- Mixes quantitative evidence (statistics, benchmarks) with qualitative examples (case studies, company examples)
- Builds cumulatively -- each piece of evidence strengthens the previous one
- Presents data in context (percentages mean more when the baseline is clear)

**What makes it bad:**
- Cherry-picks data that supports the thesis while ignoring contradictions
- Cites data without attribution or context
- Relies solely on one type of evidence (all stats, no examples, or vice versa)
- Overwhelms the reader with data dumps instead of weaving evidence into narrative

**Transition phrases:**
- "The numbers bear this out."
- "Consider [Company]'s experience."
- "Research from [Source] reinforces this point."

---

### 5. Counterpoint
<!-- advisory-only -->

**Purpose:** Acknowledges and addresses the strongest opposing views. Demonstrates intellectual honesty and strengthens the argument by engaging with objections rather than ignoring them.

**Typical length:** 1-2 paragraphs (roughly 10-15% of total word count).

**What makes it good:**
- Engages with the strongest version of the opposing argument (not a straw man)
- Acknowledges what the other side gets right before explaining where it falls short
- Uses evidence or reasoning to address the objection, not dismissal

**What makes it bad:**
- Ignores obvious objections entirely
- Sets up weak objections just to knock them down
- Concedes too much and undermines the argument
- Is dismissive or condescending toward opposing views

**Transition phrases:**
- "The obvious objection is..."
- "Critics will point out that..."
- "There's a reasonable case for [alternative view], but..."

---

### 6. Commentary
<!-- advisory-only -->

**Purpose:** Integrates expert voices and industry perspectives to add authority and texture. These are the voices of practitioners, analysts, and leaders who lend credibility to the argument.

**Typical length:** 1-2 paragraphs (roughly 10-15% of total word count). Commentary may also be woven throughout other sections.

**What makes it good:**
- Features recognised experts with relevant domain authority
- Uses quotes that add insight, not just agreement ("As [Expert] puts it, '...'")
- Includes diverse perspectives -- not just the usual suspects
- Integrates quotes into the narrative flow rather than dropping them in as standalone blocks

**What makes it bad:**
- Uses quotes that merely restate what the author already said
- Relies on unknown or irrelevant sources
- Piles up quotes without connecting them to the argument
- Includes quotes purely for name-dropping

**Integration patterns:**
- "As [Name], [Role] at [Company], puts it: '[quote].'"
- "[Name] of [Company] has argued that [paraphrase], noting that '[direct quote].'"

---

### 7. Conclusion
<!-- advisory-only -->

**Purpose:** Synthesis and forward-looking implications. Tells the reader what to take away and what to watch for next.

**Typical length:** 1-2 paragraphs (roughly 5-10% of total word count).

**What makes it good:**
- Does not simply repeat the argument -- synthesises it with the evidence presented
- Points forward: what should the reader do, watch for, or think about differently
- Ends with conviction, not a whimper

**What makes it bad:**
- Merely summarises what was already said
- Introduces new arguments or evidence not previously discussed
- Ends with a vague platitude ("Only time will tell")
- Trails off without a clear final point

**Example closing patterns:**
- "The companies that [action] now will [outcome]. The rest will be playing catch-up."
- "The question is no longer whether [trend] will happen, but who will be ready when it does."

## Conventions
<!-- advisory-only -->

### Headline Conventions

- Sentence case, not title case
- Specific over clever: "Why MMM adoption stalls at the 18-month mark" beats "The MMM mirage"
- 8-14 words preferred. Subheads / decks may carry up to 20 words to clarify the angle
- Avoid clickbait constructions ("You won't believe...", "The one thing...")
- A working headline appears in the brief; the final headline is set at the end of editing

### Lede Style

This content type favours one of three lede patterns:

- **Data-led:** Open with a concrete, surprising statistic. ("Last quarter, X fell by 30% — and most of the industry hasn't noticed yet.")
- **Event-led:** Anchor to a recent industry move. ("When [Company] announced [move] last week, the reaction was [X]. But the real story is [Y].")
- **Provocation-led:** Open with a falsifiable claim that some readers will disagree with. ("The conventional wisdom about [topic] is wrong, and the data proves it.")

Avoid scene-setting ledes, dictionary-definition ledes, and throat-clearing ("In recent years..."). The lede must commit to a specific argument inside two sentences. If it could open any article on the topic, it is too generic.

### Visual Callouts

This content type supports the following visual callouts. Use sparingly -- one or two per piece, never as decoration.

- **Pull quote:** A single sentence (under 25 words) drawn from the body, used to break the column visually and reinforce the core argument
- **Data callout:** A standalone statistic with attribution (number, what it measures, source, date). Used when a single data point anchors the argument
- **Sidebar:** A short (50-150 word) explanatory aside for a concept that would derail the main flow if explained inline. Used for definitions, methodology notes, or worked examples

### Resolution Style

How the piece resolves at the very end. Choose one (and only one) per article:

- **CTA:** A specific action the reader should take next ("Re-run your last incrementality test against the new baseline.")
- **Takeaway:** A crystallised one-sentence implication ("The companies treating measurement as compliance are about to be outpaced by the ones treating it as competitive advantage.")
- **Open question:** A pointed question that frames what the reader should watch for ("So the question isn't whether MMM is ready -- it's whether your team is.")

Avoid mixed resolutions (a CTA plus a takeaway plus a question reads as hedging).

### Attribution Style

- All data must include the source name and date (e.g., "according to Gartner's 2025 report")
- Prefer primary sources over aggregators or secondary coverage
- When citing research, note methodology or sample size if it is relevant to credibility
- For quotes, always include the speaker's name, title, and organisation
- If a source is paywalled or hard to access, note the publication name and date so it can be located

### Data Presentation

- Integrate statistics into the narrative rather than listing them in isolation
- Provide context for numbers: percentages need baselines, growth figures need timeframes
- Use round numbers and plain language where precision is not critical ("nearly half" vs "47.3%")
- When referencing charts or visual data, describe the key takeaway rather than saying "see chart"
- Avoid data dumps -- each data point should serve the argument

### Quote Integration

- Weave quotes into the flow of the argument; do not drop them in as standalone blocks
- Use quotes to add insight or authority the author cannot provide alone
- Paraphrase when the speaker's exact words are less important than their point
- Limit direct quotes to moments where the speaker's phrasing is particularly sharp, memorable, or authoritative
- Attribute clearly on first reference: full name, title, organisation

### Tone Guidance

- Authoritative but not arrogant: the writer has a point of view and defends it with evidence
- Accessible but not dumbed down: assumes the reader has domain knowledge, avoids unnecessary jargon explanation
- Evidence-based: claims are supported, not asserted
- Direct: short sentences, active voice, concrete language preferred over abstract
- Confident but honest: acknowledge uncertainty where it exists rather than overstating

### What Distinguishes Trade Media from General Business Journalism
<!-- advisory-only -->

- Trade media assumes domain expertise -- it does not explain basic industry concepts
- The audience reads for practical implications, not entertainment
- Arguments are expected to be specific and actionable, not high-level trend commentary
- Sources and data carry more weight than narrative flair
- The writer is expected to have (or convincingly simulate) insider knowledge of the industry
- Trade media tolerates more technical language and industry-specific terminology than mainstream business press
