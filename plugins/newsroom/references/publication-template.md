# Acme Analytics Publication Config

> This is an example publication config using a fictional brand. Copy this file, rename it to your brand (e.g., `your-brand.md`), and replace the content with your own.

## Mission

One sentence describing why this publication exists -- the editorial reason for being. Example: "Acme Analytics exists to give marketing leaders evidence-based perspective on measurement, so they can defend marketing investment with the same rigour finance defends capital allocation."

## Brand Voice

Confident and clear, like a sharp colleague who reads widely and explains well. Acme Analytics writes with authority grounded in evidence — never bluster. The tone is professional but human: we use "we" and "you" naturally, avoid corporate stiffness, and aren't afraid of a well-placed analogy. Technical depth is calibrated to the audience — we respect their intelligence without assuming they have a PhD in statistics. Data is woven into narrative, never dumped.

Key attributes:
- **Authoritative** — we back claims with evidence, not opinion
- **Accessible** — complex ideas explained simply, jargon defined on first use
- **Direct** — short sentences when a point is clear, longer ones when nuance demands it
- **Engaged** — we care about the reader's problems, not just our expertise

## Audience

Senior marketing leaders at mid-to-enterprise companies (250+ employees). Primarily:

- **CMOs and VPs of Marketing** — responsible for strategy and budget allocation. They care about demonstrating ROI to the C-suite and board. Familiar with marketing concepts, comfortable with data, but not statisticians.
- **Marketing directors and heads of performance** — hands-on leaders who manage campaigns and teams. They want actionable frameworks, not just theory.
- **Marketing ops and analytics leads** — technically proficient, interested in methodology and implementation detail.

Common concerns: proving marketing's value, allocating budget effectively, navigating measurement challenges (privacy, signal loss), staying ahead of industry shifts.

## Tone Rules

Explicit always / never lists with examples. These are guardrails the Editor and Journalist apply at draft time and during revision.

### Always

- **Lead with the reader's problem.** "If your CFO is asking why marketing spend isn't paying back, here's how to answer." Not: "In today's complex marketing landscape..."
- **Attribute every claim.** "Forrester's 2025 survey shows..." Not: "Studies show..."
- **Use the active voice for assertion.** "We argue..." Not: "It could be argued that..."

### Never

- **Never open with a definition.** No "Marketing mix modelling, or MMM, is..."
- **Never use unsupported superlatives.** No "best-in-class", "world-leading", "revolutionary".
- **Never editorialise on competitors by name** unless the editorial point requires it and the framing is fair.

## Topical Scope

### In scope

- Marketing measurement, attribution, incrementality
- Budget allocation and marketing finance
- Adtech and martech shifts that change measurement
- Practitioner case studies in measurement and effectiveness

### Out of scope

- Creative critique unrelated to measurement
- General leadership / management advice
- Vendor comparisons that read as buyer guides
- Tactical channel-specific how-tos (e.g., "how to optimise Meta CPMs")

## Content Pillars

1. **Marketing measurement and effectiveness** — How to measure what works, attribution vs incrementality, evolving measurement approaches in a privacy-first world.
2. **Budget optimisation** — How to allocate resources for maximum impact, scenario planning, diminishing returns analysis.
3. **Data-driven marketing leadership** — Making defensible decisions, communicating results to non-technical stakeholders, building a measurement culture.
4. **Industry trends and market shifts** — What's changing in adtech, martech, and media, and what it means for marketers.
5. **Practitioner perspectives** — Case studies, expert interviews, real-world applications of marketing science.

## Terminology

### Preferred Terms

- "Marketing effectiveness" over "marketing performance" (broader, less channel-specific)
- "Investment" over "spend" when discussing marketing budgets (frames marketing as a growth lever, not a cost)
- "Evidence-based" over "data-driven" (acknowledges qualitative judgment alongside data)
- "Optimise" not "optimize" (Australian English)
- "Modelling" not "modeling" (Australian English)
- "Incrementality" — always define on first use as "the additional outcome caused by marketing activity"
- "Marketing mix modelling (MMM)" — spell out on first use, then abbreviation is fine

### Terms to Avoid

- "Growth hacking" — misaligned with evidence-based positioning
- "Vanity metrics" — too dismissive, prefer "awareness metrics" or "top-of-funnel indicators"
- "Best-in-class" — overused, empty without evidence
- "Disruptive" / "revolutionary" — unsubstantiated superlatives
- "Synergy" — corporate jargon with no precise meaning
- Competitor product names unless editorially necessary and clearly contextualised

### Jargon Policy

Marketing terminology is fair game — our audience knows what CPM, ROAS, and CLV mean. Statistical and analytics terminology should be defined on first use with a brief parenthetical or a one-sentence explanation. If a term appears more than 3 times in a piece, define it once early and use freely after that.

Examples:
- OK without definition: ROI, CPM, CPA, funnel, attribution, A/B testing
- Define on first use: incrementality, Bayesian inference, marginal returns, multi-touch attribution (MTA), media mix modelling (MMM)

## Style Rules

- **Headings:** Sentence case ("How marketing mix modelling works", not "How Marketing Mix Modelling Works")
- **Numbers:** Spell out one through nine, numerals for 10 and above. Always use numerals for percentages (5%, not five percent) and money ($2.3M)
- **Dates:** Day Month Year, no ordinals (15 March 2026, not March 15th, 2026)
- **Oxford comma:** Yes, always
- **Paragraphs:** Short — 2-4 sentences max for digital readability
- **Subheadings:** Every 150-250 words to aid scanning
- **Citations:** Inline links preferred. For data claims, include source and year in parentheses: (Gartner, 2025)
- **Article openings:** Lead with the tension or insight, not background. Hook the reader in the first sentence.
- **Article closings:** End with implication or action, not summary. "So what?" is the test.
- **Emphasis:** Bold for key terms on introduction. Italics sparingly for nuance. Never ALL CAPS.

## Byline, Sign-off, and CTA Conventions

- **Byline format:** "By [First Last], [Role] at Acme Analytics." Always real humans, never "The Acme Team."
- **Sign-off:** None. The piece ends on the closing argument, not on a "thanks for reading" note.
- **CTA conventions:** A single closing CTA, integrated into the final paragraph rather than appended. Acceptable forms: an invitation to read a related piece, a question to provoke discussion, or a pointer to a relevant Acme resource. Never a sales pitch.

## Distribution Context

Where this publication's articles run, and what that implies for framing.

- **Primary channel:** Acme Analytics blog (own channel). Pieces should assume the reader landed via an industry mention, newsletter, or search -- not a cold scroll.
- **Trade media placement:** Selected articles are placed with industry trade outlets (e.g., Marketing Week, AdExchanger). When flagged for trade placement, neutralise Acme-specific framing and lead with the industry argument, not the brand POV.
- **Syndication:** Pieces may be repurposed in the monthly Acme newsletter and on LinkedIn (long-form). Avoid format-specific tics that would not translate.
- **Mix:** Roughly 70% own channel, 25% trade placement, 5% syndication-first.

## Required Disclosures and Compliance Notes

- Disclose Acme commercial relationships when naming partner vendors. Standard form: "(Acme is a [partner / customer / investor in] [Vendor].)"
- When citing Acme proprietary data, name the dataset and the time window. Do not cite without naming.
- Avoid forward-looking financial claims about public companies (legal flagged this in 2024). Stick to reported figures or analyst estimates with attribution.
- Health / financial-services adjacent topics: route to legal review before publication.
